REV BEAT · EPS BEAT · MARGIN MISS · NO DIVIDEND

Himalayan Reinsurance delivered its fourth full year of profitable operations post-incorporation, posting revenue of Rs 7.73B (+16.6% YoY) and net profit of Rs 1.39B (+8.3%). Revenue beat consensus expectations driven by premium expansion in both domestic treaty and international facultative books. Gross margin, however, compressed to 33.5% from 36.5% — indicating rising claim costs and administrative expenses outpacing top-line growth. No dividend was declared vs Rs 4.74/share the previous year, disappointing income-focused retail holders. The stock price stood at Rs 922.78 near results date, implying a 72× P/E — a multiple that began unwinding sharply in early 2026. The April 1–5 policy selloff saw HRL hit a −10% lower circuit on April 5 with Rs 582.54M turnover — the highest single-stock turnover on NEPSE that day — signalling institutional-scale distribution. Q2 FY2082/83 results (latest available) show annualized EPS of Rs 11.92, slightly below the full-year Rs 12.79, suggesting the earnings growth rate is moderating entering the new fiscal year.

CATALYSTS & RISKS
🚀 CATALYSTS & UPSIDE DRIVERS
International Expansion: Already operating in 52 countries. Diversification beyond Nepal's mandatory cession reduces regulatory concentration. New market entries in South/Southeast Asia could materially grow the premium pool in FY2083/84 and beyond.
Nepal Insurance Market Growth: New life and non-life insurers continue to receive licenses. Every new insurer increases HRL's captive cession pool as the sole private-sector domestic reinsurer — a durable structural tailwind.
Strong Promoter Network: Backed by Nepal Bank Ltd., Rastriya Banijya Bank, IME Group, Golchha Group, Shanker Group, and ~20 other leading conglomerates. Institutional support provides deep capital access and distribution advantages.
Post-Unlock Re-rating Potential: Once Dec 2026 promoter unlock is absorbed and true price discovery occurs, valuations could reset to reflect fundamental value — potentially creating a long-term entry opportunity for patient investors.
T&M / Operational Build-Out: Investment in underwriting technology, retrocession infrastructure, and talent could improve combined ratios and support margin recovery. International rating upgrades would unlock lower-cost retrocession capacity.
⚠️ RISKS & DOWNSIDE FACTORS
Promoter Unlock Overhang (Dec 27, 2026): 70% of all shares (7.6 crore units) become tradeable in ~8 months. NEPSE's thin daily liquidity (~Rs 5–10B market-wide) cannot absorb even partial institutional exit without severe price dislocation. April 2026 circuit-breakers foreshadow this risk.
Regulatory Cession Decline: Mandatory direct cession reduces 2% per annum. The core revenue moat that powered HRL's early growth is structurally shrinking. Voluntary international business must accelerate to fill the gap — execution risk is high.
Seismic / HPAI Catastrophe Risk: Nepal sits in seismic zone IV-V. The 2015 earthquake killed 9,000+. Catastrophe reserves of Rs 7.7Cr (~$578K) are inadequate relative to aggregate reinsurance exposure across Nepal's entire insurance industry.
Equity Portfolio Concentration: Investment income depends heavily on NEPSE equity performance. When NEPSE falls (as in April 2026 −105 points in a day), HRL's investment portfolio, insurance fund value, and book value all compress simultaneously.
Valuation Risk at P/E 59×: Global reinsurance peers (Swiss Re, Munich Re) trade at 10–16× earnings. HRL at 59× requires sustained 25%+ EPS CAGR for 5 years to justify current price — an aggressive assumption given regulatory headwinds and margin compression.

Himalayan Reinsurance Limited is a genuinely pioneering institution — Nepal's first private-sector reinsurer, consistently profitable since inception, expanding into 52 countries with strong government bank backing and internationally affirmed credit ratings (ICRA A–, AM Best B+). Revenue growth of 16.6% and EPS of Rs 12.79 in FY2024/25 demonstrate a real and growing business. However, the investment case is materially encumbered by a P/E of ~59× (vs global peers at 10–18×), a P/BV of 4.49×, no dividend declared in FY25, ongoing gross margin compression (−301bps), and — most critically — 7.6 crore promoter shares unlocking on December 27, 2026, representing 70% of all listed shares. The April 2026 policy selloff, which triggered a −10% lower circuit with Rs 582M single-day turnover (NEPSE's highest), demonstrated precisely how thin the market is and how rapidly institutional exit can overwhelm retail buyers. Catastrophe reserve adequacy is also a structural concern given Nepal's seismic exposure. The fundamental growth story is intact and real — but the price does not offer adequate margin of safety. New investors should await post-unlock price stabilisation and P/E normalisation toward the 20–30× range. Existing holders should size risk carefully and set stop-loss discipline ahead of the December 2026 unlock event.
